        Nieves Fernandez

                                                    1906 - 1997

Nieves Fernandez worked as a teacher in Tacloban. Her life was disrupted when Japan invaded the Philippines in 1941. Her pupils refer to her as “Miss Fernandez,” and she was very caring towards them. Her intense maternal instincts emerged when the Japanese menaced to murder her students. She transformed from a nurturing school teacher to a covert solitary assassin, recognized for eliminating over 200 Japanese soldiers during World War II

Upon their arrival in the Philippines in 1941, the Japanese seized all the belongings of Filipinos. Ownership of businesses was prohibited, and teaching was restricted to topics sanctioned by Imperial Japan.

In Tacloban, her home city, the Japanese compelled business owners to comply by pouring boiling water on them.

“When the Japs came, no one could keep anything,” Fernandez told a reporter from the Lewiston Daily Sun in November 1944. “They took everything they wanted.”

Fernandez chose to take action herself after the Japanese confiscated her belongings and minor enterprise and threatened to also seize her students.

Fernandez earned the nickname “The Silent Killer.” Dressed entirely in black and by herself, she would prepare ambushes in the jungle with only a homemade shotgun crafted from a gas pipe and her bolo.

For two and a half years, Fernandez executed ambushes by herself. She would venture into the jungle without shoes, taking down numerous enemy soldiers by herself.

Eventually, her heroics inspired a following among Tacloban’s men. Fernandez shifted from teaching the alphabet to schoolchildren to training men how to kill silently. From being called “Miss Fernandez” by her pupils, she earned the title “Captain Fernandez” among the 110 guerrillas under her command.

Her small guerrilla army became so efficient and deadly, the Americans were astounded that a woman led them. In fact, Fernandez was the only female guerrilla commander in the Philippines during World War II.

The Japanese became so tired of Fernandez that they chose to place a P10,000 reward on her head, hoping that other Filipinos would turn her in.

However, nobody did.

During the conflict, Fernandez and her company of 110 would free prisoners of war, disrupt Japanese supplies, and carry out numerous attacks on the Japanese Imperial Army in the Philippines.

Toward the end of World War II and when the Americans arrived on Leyte in 1944, Fernandez and her guerrilla forces had already liberated many villages from the Japanese and freed dozens of comfort women.

Below, an article from the Associated Press published in 1944 is the only surviving record of Fernandez’s heroics during World War II.

School-Ma’am Led Guerrillas on Leyte

Woman Tells How She Helped “Gas Pipe Gang” Slay 200 Japanese

LEYTE, Oct. 26-(Delayed)—A prime former school teacher, so far as known here the only woman to fight two and a half years with Filipino guerrillas, told today how she commanded 110 natives who killed 200 Japanese with shotguns made from sections of gas pipes.

"That was when they called me Captain Nieves Fernandez, she said. "Now I'm just Miss Fernandez."

Paler than most native women of this section (her first name, Nieves, is the Spanish word for snows), Miss Fernandez was without shoes and was attired in a plain black frock as she conferred with American officers. She is 38 years old "at present."

After teaching school at Tacloban. the Leyte capital recently occupied by American forces invading the Japanese-held Philippines., Miss Fernandez said she developed a wholesale business of her own.

“But when the Japs came,” she related, "No one could keep anything. They took everything they wanted. They had ways of persuading, like giving you scalding hot baths and freezing cold baths alternately, with never a rest, never any food, never any water except the soapy water in the baths.”

Working with guerrillas south of Tacloban, Miss Fernandez rounded up native men to resist the Japanese. These men, she said, had three American rifles. The rest they made themselves out of gas pipe. They loaded them with gunpowder and old nails. They also made grenades, and sometimes they got hold of Japanese weapons.

U.S. Intelligence officers said the Japanese offered 10,000 pesos for her head. She was wounded once. There is a bullet scar on her right forearm.

(Many Filipinos are experts at making effective guns out of gas pipe, The weapons are as deadly as any first-rate shotgun. The home-made guns are called "latongs" in the Visayan dialect of the Central Philippines, and “paltik” in the Tagalog dialect of the Manila area. In some of the tougher areas of the Manila district, residences must be guarded not because housebreakers want money or jewels but because they will strip the house of gas pipes for the illicit shotgun industry.)

WATCH & LEARN



  • Nieves Fernandez was the only known female guerrilla leader in the Philippines during World War II who personally led soldiers in combat against Japanese forces.
  • She trained local fighters in the use of improvised weapons, including a specially designed shotgun and homemade grenades, despite having no formal military training.
  • After the war, she returned to her profession as a schoolteacher in Tacloban, showing her dedication to education even after becoming a war hero.
