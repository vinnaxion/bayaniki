             Antonio Luna

                                                    1866 – 1899

General Antonio Luna's life was a story of greatness. He was loyal and an extremely firm believer in military discipline. However, many soldiers who did not understand the meaning of military discipline grew to resent him, and this resentment ultimately led to his untimely death — he was not killed by American soldiers, but by his own fellow military men. Antonio Luna was born in Urbistondo, Binondo, Manila on October 29, 1866. His parents were both Ilocanos, and he was the younger brother of Juan Luna, the greatest Filipino painter. From his earliest years, Antonio showed an exceptional intellect and a fierce dedication to excellence.

The intelligent young man finished his degree in pharmacy at the University of Santo Tomás. He then continued his studies abroad, specializing in his field at a clinic in Belgium, and later worked as an assistant to Dr. Lattaux in his laboratory in Paris. Two years after returning to the Philippines, Luna was accused of involvement in the Cry of Pugad Lawin. Together with other Filipino leaders, he was exiled to Spain. Rather than accepting defeat, Luna used his time in exile to deepen his commitment to the Filipino movement for freedom, continuing to study and prepare himself for the struggle that lay ahead.

When he returned to the Philippines, Antonio Luna was appointed Commanding General of the Philippine Army. He became the most feared military commander in the Philippine-American War, known for his strict discipline, tactical brilliance, and fierce defense of the country. He organized elite units, established a military academy, and built a defense line that challenged American forces across Luzon. Despite his greatness, his uncompromising standards created enemies within his own ranks. On June 5, 1899, he was assassinated by soldiers of his own army. He is remembered to this day as the Greatest Filipino Soldier of the Revolution — the Fiery General.

WATCH & LEARN



  • Before becoming a general, Antonio Luna was a trained pharmacist and scientist who worked in laboratories in both Belgium and Paris.
  • His older brother Juan Luna was the celebrated Filipino painter behind the iconic masterpiece "Spoliarium." Antonio's fiery personality and uncompromising military standards earned him both fierce loyalty and bitter enemies.
  • His life story inspired the acclaimed 2015 Filipino film "Heneral Luna," which brought his legacy to a new generation of Filipinos.
