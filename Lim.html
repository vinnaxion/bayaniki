           Vicente P. Lim

                                                    1888 - 1944

Vicente P. Lim was born in Calamba, Philippines, in February of 1888. At the end of the Spanish-American War, he was a student at the Philippine Normal School in Manila. Lim's outstanding intellectual and athletic skills were noted by an American teacher at the school, who suggested that he apply for a scholarship to West Point. Lim applied and was accepted in 1910 after taking his professor's suggestion.

Lim encountered a different community in the United States than he had in the Philippines. Since the United States had recently taken possession of the Philippines in 1898, Lim experienced prejudice at the Academy because of his ethnic heritage. Despite the difficulties, he refused to let the other cadets intimidate him, and in the end, he gained their respect by performing exceptionally well in both his academic and martial training. He graduated from West Point as the first Filipino-American in 1914.

After finishing further training in Europe, Lim was deployed to the Philippines as World War I started. He was subsequently assigned to the Philippine Scouts, a unit made up mainly of Filipino-Americans. Lim devoted a significant portion of his Army career to training and organizing the Philippine Scouts to transform them into a strong combat unit. While advancing in his career, he participated in additional military education courses throughout the United States and concentrated his studies on a potential conflict with Japan. Lim dedicated his time in the United States to promoting the rights of Filipino-American soldiers, which put him in conflict with other American officers.

After the establishment of the semi-autonomous Philippine Commonwealth in 1935, Lim chose to pursue the rank of brigadier general in the Philippine Army. This newly created unit was still a part of the United States military then, but it had to function with a limited budget. Lim passionately advocated for improved gear and training for his troops, while aiming to promote distinct Philippine military customs. With the threat of war in the Pacific in 1941, Lim abandoned his desk position to take charge of the Philippine 41st Division in the field. After the assault on Pearl Harbor, the 41st was sent to the Bataan Peninsula in preparation for a Japanese invasion of the Philippines

Japanese troops reached Bataan in January 1942 and encountered strong resistance from American and Filipino forces. Lim's 41st Division fended off Japanese attacks and stopped them from surrounding adjacent American troops. Carlos Romulo, a lieutenant of Gen. MacArthur, sent a message to Lim: “[You are] the symbol of the Philippine Army in Bataan: exhausted, famished, gravely ill, pushed back repeatedly – yet unconquerable!” Following months of intense combat, the combined American-Filipino unit was vanquished, and Lim was captured by the Japanese in April 1942.

Lim endured the notorious Bataan Death March and was freed after pledging not to oppose the Japanese military government. As a key player in the Philippine nationalist movement, the Japanese-supported puppet regime sought to persuade Lim to openly endorse Japanese dominance over the Philippines. Lim told them he would prefer to die than betray his nation. To escape notice, Lim pretended to be sick and was taken to the Philippine Cancer Institute, where he stayed for several months.

From his hospital room, Lim consistently organized, funded, and directed multiple groups of resistance fighters opposing the Japanese. Lim organized insurgent assaults on Japanese installations and communicated with Allied troops to share crucial information regarding Japanese military movements. In 1944, military planners in the United States devised a plan to extract key Philippine resistance leaders – among them Lim. On his journey to an American submarine, Lim was seized by Japanese troops who accused him of being a leader of the resistance. Following a short trial, Lim was put to death in Bilibid Prison.

Lim received the Legion of Merit posthumously from the American military and numerous accolades from the Philippine military. He is celebrated as a national hero in the Philippines. In addition to his military service, Lim is recognized for establishing the Filipino Boy Scouts Association and for being a lifelong supporter of Philippine independence.

WATCH & LEARN



  • Vicente Lim was the first Filipino graduate of the United States Military Academy, making him a pioneer among Filipino military officers.
  • He became one of the first Filipino generals in the Philippine Army and played an important role in strengthening the country’s military system before World War II.
  • During World War II, he joined the resistance movement against Japanese occupation and was later captured and executed, becoming a symbol of bravery and sacrifice.
