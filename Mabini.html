        Apolinario Mabini

                                                    1864 – 1903

Apolinario Mabini was a man of sublime ideals who devoted his life to seeking the freedom of his country. He was a courageous patriot, a wise philosopher, and a learned statesman. His paralysis did not hinder him from working tirelessly to gain the country's independence. Apolinario Mabini was born into a poor family on July 23, 1864. His father was Inocencio Mabini, a Barangay Captain, and his mother was Dionisia Maranan, the daughter of a village teacher. From his early years, Mabini loved to study, preferring books over play, and he excelled in their little barrio school despite the many challenges that poverty placed in his path.

Because of poverty, Mabini took on odd jobs to help his parents send him to school. He eventually finished law at the University of Santo Tomás. There is a memorable story about his graduation: he had given up hope of attending the rites because he had no money to buy a cap, hood, and gown. However, a big gift package containing exactly those items was sent to him by a grateful woman whose legal case he had once helped with in court. When Emilio Aguinaldo heard of Mabini's brilliant mind, he appointed him as his Chief Adviser. From then on, the paralyzed Mabini was carried by hammock wherever Aguinaldo needed him.

Mabini wrote the True Decalogue and helped lay the foundations of the First Philippine Republic. He earned the enduring title "Brain of the Revolution." In 1899, Mabini resigned as Aguinaldo's adviser due to poor health. He was captured by the Americans and exiled to Guam on January 7, 1901. He endured his exile with dignity and continued to write for his country. He returned to the Philippines in 1903 but died of cholera on May 13, 1903, at the age of 39. He is remembered as the Sublime Paralytic.

WATCH & LEARN



  • Apolinario Mabini finished his law degree at the University of Santo Tomás despite extreme poverty, relying on his brilliance and the kindness of those he had helped.
  • He proposed the establishment of a Filipino national church independent of Spanish friars, an idea that later inspired the founding of the Iglesia Filipina Independiente, also known as the Aglipayan Church.
  • His face appears on the Philippine ten-peso coin, and several municipalities across the country bear his name in his honor.
