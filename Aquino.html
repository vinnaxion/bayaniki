        Melchora Aquino

                                                    1812 - 1919

Melchora Aquino, also known as Melchora Aquino de Ramos and widely remembered as “Tandang Sora,” was one of the most respected and inspiring figures during the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonization. She was born on January 6, 1812, in Banlat, Caloocan, into a humble farming family. Her parents, Juan Aquino and Valentina de Aquino, raised her with strong values despite their limited resources. Because of poverty, Melchora did not receive formal schooling, but she became known for her natural intelligence, wisdom, and strong sense of compassion. As she grew older, she worked as a farmer and vendor, supporting herself and her family through hard work. She married Fulgencio Ramos, a cabeza de barangay (village leader), and together they had several children. However, after her husband’s death, she was left to raise her children on her own, showing her strength and independence early in life.

During the late 19th century, the Philippines was experiencing growing unrest under Spanish rule. Many Filipinos were beginning to resist oppression, leading to the formation of the Katipunan, a secret revolutionary society founded by Andres Bonifacio. At this time, Melchora Aquino was already in her 80s, yet she chose to support the cause of independence. Her home, located near Balintawak, became a meeting place for Katipunan members. It was also a safe refuge for revolutionaries who were wounded, tired, or being pursued by Spanish authorities. She provided them with food, shelter, medical care, and encouragement, treating them like her own children. Because of this, the revolutionaries began calling her “Tandang Sora,” a term of respect meaning “Old Mother Sora.”

Aquino played an important role during the early days of the revolution, especially during events such as the Cry of Pugad Lawin, where Filipino revolutionaries declared their resistance against Spain. Her home was said to be a place where Katipuneros gathered before and after important meetings. She not only provided physical support but also boosted the morale of the fighters by offering advice and comfort. At a time when many people were afraid of punishment, Aquino showed courage by openly helping the revolutionaries despite the risks. Her actions proved that the fight for freedom was not limited to soldiers alone, but also involved ordinary citizens who believed in the cause.

Because of her involvement, Spanish authorities eventually discovered her connection to the Katipunan. She was arrested and interrogated, but she refused to reveal any information about the revolutionaries. Despite her old age, she remained firm and loyal, refusing to betray those she had helped. As punishment, the Spanish exiled her to Guam. During her exile, she endured separation from her family and homeland, but she remained strong and faithful to her beliefs. Her exile became a symbol of sacrifice and unwavering loyalty to the Filipino cause.

After the defeat of Spain in 1898, Aquino was allowed to return to the Philippines. She came back as a respected figure, admired by many for her bravery and kindness during the revolution. Although she did not seek fame or recognition, her contributions were widely acknowledged by both revolutionaries and ordinary citizens. She chose to live a quiet and simple life after returning, staying away from politics and public attention. She passed away on March 2, 1919, at the age of 107, making her one of the oldest Filipino heroes. Her long life allowed her to witness major changes in Philippine history, from Spanish rule to the early years of American colonization.

Melchora Aquino is often called the “Mother of the Philippine Revolution.” Many places in the Philippines are named after her, including Tandang Sora Avenue and the Tandang Sora district in Quezon City. Her story is taught in schools as an example of patriotism, compassion, and courage. Unlike many heroes who fought with weapons, Aquino showed that kindness, care, and moral support can also play a powerful role in shaping history. Her life reminds students that anyone, regardless of age, gender, or social status, can contribute to the nation’s struggle for freedom.

Her role in history also highlights the importance of women in the Philippine Revolution. While many historical accounts focus on male leaders, figures like Melchora Aquino prove that women were equally important in supporting and sustaining the movement. She became a symbol not only of resistance but also of unity, as she brought together revolutionaries from different backgrounds and treated them as one family. Her courage inspired others to continue the fight, even during difficult times.

WATCH & LEARN



  • Melchora Aquino was already over 80 years old when she actively supported the Katipunan, making her one of the oldest participants in the revolution.
  • She lived to be 107 years old, which is why many Filipinos also remember her as a symbol of strength and long life. Because of her motherly care, Katipuneros trusted her deeply and often turned to her for both physical help and emotional support during the revolution.
